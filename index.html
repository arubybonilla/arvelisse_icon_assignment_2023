Food insecurity in New York City: its impact to Latinx communities after COVID-19

BY ARVELISSE BONILLA RAMOS

Photo by Arvelisse Bonilla Ramos

In 2021, 33.8 million people lived in food-insecure households. Around 521,000 children lived in households in which one or more children experienced very low food security across the United States. Latinxs remain disproportionately impacted by food insecurity as food insecurity continues to unfairly impact racial and ethnic minorities. With COVID-19 restrictions easing throughout New York, many are still working towards a stable economic environment. Many variables in place are still impacting including a high price crisis that has sustained past the summer, and COVID-19’s impact on agriculture and production. Some of the causes of food insecurity include: poverty, unemployment, or low income, lack of affordable housing, chronic health conditions or lack of access to healthcare, systemic racism and racial discrimination.

While about one-third of native-born New York City households have inadequate income, 37% of naturalized citizens and 51% of non-citizens lack adequate income. Use of emergency food services increased among most New Yorkers during the pandemic and has for many a necessity, for example community fridges, SNAP, food banks and others. More than one in five SNAP participants are Latinxs. Latinxs also make up more than 40% of WIC participants across the country, according to the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) latest annual report.

FAMILY

In 2021, 18.5% of Latinx children experienced food insecurity. Across the nation, 5 million children lived in food-insecure households in which children — along with adults — were food insecure.

MONEY

NYC's Human Resources Administration (HRA) allocated a $12.5M budget for emergency food assistance. The planned budget for the 2022 fiscal year total of $27.7M for emergency food reserve.

FOOD ASSISTANCE

HRA’s revamped its emergency food program and named it Community Food Connection (CFC) (formerly known as the Emergency Food Assistance program). The program wants to address food insecurity in New York City and ensuring that low-income communities have access to fresh and healthy food. More than 250 food banks participate in the CFC program.

LATINXS ACROSS THE NATION

1 in 6 Latinx individuals currently experience food insecurity. Latinx people are 2.5 more likely to suffer food insecurity tan white individuals in the country. Latinx children are more than twice as likely to face hunger as white children. In 2021, 18.5% experienced food insecurity.

ACCESS TO EMERGENCY PROGRAMS IN NYC

In April of 2020, 38% of Latinx New Yorkers were almost twice as likely to report having less access to emergency food services due to COVID-19, compared just 19% of White New Yorkers.
